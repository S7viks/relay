\begin{abstract}
Modern AI deployments rely on multiple large language models, retrieval pipelines, and autonomous agents, yet orchestrate them through ad-hoc scripts that lack principled mechanisms for model selection, quality assurance, or cross-provider coordination. We present the Global Artificial Intelligence Operating Layer, a layered orchestration framework that coordinates heterogeneous large language model providers behind a uniform interface, decomposes complex queries into parallelizable subtasks, and aggregates multi-model responses through a novel consensus mechanism. The architecture supports federated data access, cross-organizational governance, and shared-state management via dedicated extension points; however, a full evaluation of these features falls outside the scope of this paper. The central algorithmic contribution is the adaptive Bayesian trust-weighted Consensus algorithm, which maintains per-model, per-domain Beta-distributed trust variables and updates them online after each consensus round, allowing the system to learn which model excels at which task domain without manual weight tuning. On a 500-query benchmark spanning analytical reasoning, code generation, multi-step problem solving, knowledge retrieval, and creative synthesis, Global Artificial Intelligence Operating Layer achieves an overall quality score of $0.83 \pm 0.02$ (24\% above single-model baselines, 13\% above LangChain), a 95.2\% success rate, and 5 milliseconds (ms) orchestration overhead. An ablation study confirms that Adaptive Bayesian Trust-Weighted Consensus yields statistically significant gains over both uniform-weight and hand-tuned static consensus ($p < 0.01$, paired $t$-test) across all five domains, with the largest improvements in code generation (+9 percentage points) and creative synthesis (+7 percentage points).
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}
Transformer-based Large Language Models(LLMs) and foundation models have pushed the boundaries of code generation, multi-modal reasoning, and complex planning~\cite{Kotei, chen2024evolution}. Building on these capabilities, practitioners now deploy \emph{agentic} AI systems, persistent processes that invoke tools, maintain internal state, and execute multi-step workflows, transforming AI from a stateless query-response utility into a core layer of digital infrastructure. Two concurrent trends magnify the coordination challenge. First, agents are evolving from short-lived tools into long-horizon entities that accumulate interaction histories and maintain internal beliefs~\cite{Liu}. Second, AI capabilities are scattering across users, cloud providers, and organizations: the same enterprise may rely on OpenAI for language tasks, Google for vision, and an in-house model for compliance-sensitive analytics~\cite{Davenport}. Together, these trends raise a fundamental systems question: how should intelligence distributed across heterogeneous agents, models, and data sources be organized, coordinated, and governed~\cite{zhu2023intelligent}. Today's ecosystem offers no satisfactory answer. Models remain tightly coupled to vendor-specific runtimes and Application Programming Interfaces (APIs), agent frameworks impose incompatible communication protocols, and enterprise data stays locked behind organizational firewalls with no principled mechanism for cross-boundary sharing~\cite{REALM}. Orchestration logic typically lives in brittle, hand-crafted pipelines that are difficult to audit, extend, or secure. Machine Learning Operations (MLOps) platforms partially address deployment concerns, but they operate within a single organization and lack native support for federated execution, cross-cloud collaboration, or compliance-aware orchestration.

From a systems perspective, this fragmentation suggests the need for an \emph{operating-system-like abstraction} for AI, one that would manage models, agents, and data through standardized interfaces in the same spirit that traditional operating systems manage computation, memory, and inter-process communication. Today's AI systems lack such an abstraction: agents, models, and data remain loosely coupled artifacts coordinated through ad-hoc glue code. Realizing the full operating system (OS) analogy, including process-level isolation, federated memory, and cross-domain governance, is a long-term research programme. As a concrete step in that direction, this work targets the orchestration and consensus layers, which are prerequisites for the higher-level capabilities.

We propose the Global AI Operating Layer (GAIOL) to address this gap. This paper focuses on the orchestration and consensus layers; extension points for federation, governance, and shared world-state are part of the architectural design, but their full evaluation is deferred to future work. Our evaluated contributions are:
\begin{itemize}
    \item A layered orchestration architecture that coordinates heterogeneous LLM providers behind a provider-agnostic interface, with a Universal AI Protocol layer for uniform communication and adapter-based integration. The design includes provisions for federated data access and cross-organizational governance as future extensions.
    \item A global orchestrator that decomposes complex queries into parallelizable subtasks via beam search, selects models through a strategy-based routing policy, and assembles multi-model responses into coherent outputs.
    \item The Adaptive Bayesian Trust-Weighted Consensus (ABTC) algorithm, which learns per-model, per-domain reliability online via Beta-Bernoulli posterior updates, replacing static consensus weights with data-driven trust scores.
\end{itemize}

The remainder of the paper is structured as follows. Section~\ref{sec:lit} surveys related work. Section~\ref{sec:arch} presents the system architecture. Section~\ref{sec:algo} details the algorithmic framework, including the ABTC formulation. Section~\ref{sec:flow} describes the end-to-end operational flow. Section~\ref{sec:exp} reports experimental results and an ablation study. Section~\ref{sec:gaps} discusses open challenges and future work, Section~\ref{sec:limitations} states limitations, and Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:lit}
The shift from single-purpose models to persistent, multi-agent AI systems has exposed architectural gaps that no existing framework fully addresses. We organize related work along four research streams: multi-agent coordination, model routing, memory and reasoning, and governance, and identify the limitations that motivate GAIOL.

Recent surveys map a rapidly expanding body of work on LLM-powered multi-agent systems~\cite{oh2015survey, dong2024large}, spanning software development~\cite{qian2024chatdev}, collaborative problem-solving~\cite{AgentVerse_EMNLP23}, multi-agent debate for improved factuality~\cite{Cooperation_NeurIPS23}, and generative social simulation~\cite{RolePlay_Nature23}. Architectures such as DyLAN (dynamic large language model agent network)~\cite{DyLAN_ICLR24} and ProAgent~\cite{ProAgent_AAAI24} demonstrate that dynamic, proactive agent networks outperform static topologies, while domain-specific deployments like SAGE (smart home agent with grounded execution)~\cite{SAGE_AAAI24} confirm practical viability. Standardized communication is a prerequisite for scaling these systems: surveys cover emerging agent protocols~\cite{AIAgentProtocols} and interoperability requirements for cross-framework collaboration~\cite{AgentInterop}. However, all of these efforts assume agents operate within a single trust domain. None provides the resource-scheduling, security-enforcement, or policy-negotiation primitives needed when agents span organizational boundaries. While frameworks like AutoGen~\cite{AutoGen} and MetaGPT~\cite{MetaGPT} define agent roles and conversation protocols, they operate at the application layer and assume a shared, trusted execution environment. GAIOL functions as a lower-level orchestration middleware, providing provider-agnostic model registry interfaces, resource scheduling, and consensus mechanisms that can support multiple independent agent frameworks.

A parallel line of work optimizes how queries reach the right model. MasRouter (multi-agent system router)~\cite{MasRouter} trains a large language model router for multi-agent task assignment; Router-R1~\cite{RouterR1} extends routing to multi-round aggregation; and LLM-TOPLA (large language model ensemble via topological data analysis)~\cite{LLMTOPLA} maximizes ensemble diversity. These routers treat model selection as an isolated optimization problem: they improve throughput and sometimes accuracy, but they do not enforce cost budgets, audit decisions, or coordinate routing across federated deployments where data residency and organizational policies constrain which models may be invoked. GAIOL's orchestration layer subsumes routing as one component within a broader governance-aware scheduling framework. Unlike these static, single-decision routers, GAIOL embeds its IntelligentRouter in a multi-step execution pipeline, using online Bayesian updates to adapt model routes dynamically. While Router-R1~\cite{RouterR1} utilizes multi-round aggregation, its routing weights are static and learned offline, unlike the online posterior updating in GAIOL.

Reasoning techniques have advanced from chain-of-thought prompting~\cite{CoT_NeurIPS22} through tree-of-thoughts~\cite{Tree_NeurIPS23}, self-consistency~\cite{SelfConsistency_ICLR23}, least-to-most decomposition~\cite{Least2Most_EMNLP22}, and planning with world models. GAIOL's multi-step orchestration pipeline builds on these primitives. For workflow planning, R \& D Agent~\cite{RDAgent}, LaMDAgent~\cite{qian2024chatdev}, Routine~\cite{Routine}, and AFLOW~\cite{AFLOW} automate agentic workflow generation, yet each framework manages workflows within a single process boundary and offers no mechanism for shared context across agents. Furthermore, standard self-consistency~\cite{SelfConsistency_ICLR23} aggregates outputs from multiple generations of a single model using majority voting, while multi-agent debate~\cite{Cooperation_NeurIPS23} achieves consensus iteratively over multiple rounds of inter-agent discussion, incurring high latency. GAIOL's ABTC provides a one-shot, cross-provider consensus mechanism that aggregates heterogeneous model responses in a single turn. Similarly, prompt compilation frameworks like DSPy~\cite{dspy} optimize prompt pipelines through automatic offline few-shot compilation, whereas GAIOL focuses on runtime resource coordination and cross-provider execution. These are complementary: DSPy-optimized modules can be deployed directly within GAIOL's individual subtask adapters.

On the memory side, surveys of memory mechanisms for LLM-based agents~\cite{MemoryOS} describe structured management with life-cycle scheduling and multi-tier hierarchies, while MemoryBank~\cite{MemoryBank_ACL23}, recurrent memory transformers~\cite{RecurrentMemory_NeurIPS23}, and structured prompting~\cite{LongContext_ICML24} extend context retention across sessions. A consistent limitation is that these architectures manage memory for individual agents or sessions; none support federated memory coordination or cross-organizational state sharing, which GAIOL's architecture provisions for via its continuous world model (Table~\ref{tab:os_mapping}); empirical evaluation is deferred to future work.

As AI systems cross jurisdictional boundaries, governance becomes a first-order design constraint rather than an afterthought. Hacker and Holweg~\cite{FineTuningReg} propose federated compliance for fine-tuned general-purpose models, highlighting conflicts between local data-residency laws and cross-border collaboration goals. Shi et al.~\cite{FM_Governance} expose the gap between regulatory intent and technical feasibility for disclosure and auditing. Solaiman and Dennison~\cite{SafetyTaxonomy_FAccT23} analyze release methods and risk tradeoffs for generative AI systems, while constitutional AI with reinforcement learning from AI feedback offers scalable alignment through AI-generated preference labels. Existing orchestration platforms treat governance as a plugin; GAIOL's design embeds policy-driven enforcement, zero-trust identity, and system-level auditing as architectural primitives (Table~\ref{tab:os_mapping}); their full deployment and evaluation are deferred to future work.

Three interconnected limitations emerge from this survey. \emph{(i)~Orchestration and interoperability:} fragmented ecosystems and missing scheduling abstractions block large-scale, cross-organizational coordination. \emph{(ii)~Context and memory:} session-scoped memory and the absence of a shared world model prevent long-horizon, multi-agent collaboration. \emph{(iii)~Security and governance:} implicit trust assumptions and centralized policy enforcement fail in federated settings where multiple organizations must collaborate under distinct regulatory regimes. GAIOL addresses (i) through its evaluated layered architecture and orchestration; it provisions for (ii) and (iii) through its continuous world model and governance-first design, whose evaluation is deferred to future work (Table~\ref{tab:os_mapping}).

\subsection{Architectural Pattern}

GAIOL follows a layered microservices architecture organized into five tiers~\cite{qian2024chatdev}. At the top, a Presentation Layer exposes a web-based interface with modular JavaScript components for authentication, reasoning visualization, model management, and system monitoring. Requests enter through an API Gateway that handles Hyper Text Transfer Protocol (HTTP) routing, input validation, authentication middleware, and rate limiting; security policies are enforced here before any downstream processing begins.

The core of the system is its Business Logic Layer, which houses the reasoning engine, model orchestration, Retrieval Augmented Generation (RAG) processing~\cite{RAG, REALM}, and the consensus mechanisms described in Section~\ref{sec:algo}. Below it, a Data Access Layer provides multi-tenant storage with row-level isolation, vector operations for semantic search, and connection pooling. Finally, an External Integration Layer wraps provider-specific APIs (Google Gemini, Hugging Face, OpenRouter) behind a uniform adapter interface, allowing the upper layers to remain provider-agnostic.

The authentication and authorization system is split into two components. An API side middleware intercepts every request to validate tokens and manage sessions, while a Supabase-backed identity provider handles user registration, credential storage, and authentication flows. Together, they enforce role-based access control with fine-grained permissions and multi-tenant isolation.

The database layer comprises three components. A unified database client manages connection pooling, transaction handling, and query execution through a single interface. On top of it, a tenant isolation layer injects the current organizational context into every query and enforces row-level security policies, preventing accidental data leakage across tenants. A vector-search module stores high-dimensional embeddings and performs approximate nearest neighbor retrieval (e.g., Dense Passage Retrieval (DPR)~\cite{DPR}, ColBERT (contextualized late interaction over BERT)~\cite{ColBERT}) to supply the RAG subsystem with semantically relevant context.

The model orchestration layer abstracts provider heterogeneity behind a uniform execution interface. A model interface defines the contract that every provider adapter must implement: text generation, embedding creation, and capability advertisement. A model registry catalogues available models with their performance profiles and per-token pricing; the registry serves as the source of truth for downstream selection decisions.

An intelligent router maps each incoming subtask to one or more models. The router evaluates task requirements, historical performance records, cost constraints, and real-time availability. For multi-faceted queries, it decomposes the request, dispatches subtasks to different models in parallel, and merges the results. An embedding engine converts text into dense vector representations for the RAG subsystem.

A performance tracker records latency, throughput, success rate, and cost per token for every model invocation. The router consumes these statistics to refine its selection policy over time, closing an online feedback loop between execution and routing.

Provider integration follows a uniform adapter pattern. Each adapter translates a provider-specific API into the common model interface defined above. The Google Gemini adapter, for instance, maps Gemini's streaming protocol and error codes into the canonical schema, while the Hugging Face adapter supports both cloud-hosted and on-premises model endpoints. An OpenRouter adapter provides a single integration point to dozens of additional models. Because every adapter conforms to the same contract, swapping or adding a provider requires no changes to the orchestration logic. A shared response cleaner strips provider-specific formatting and normalizes outputs into a uniform structure before they reach the consensus layer.

An orchestrator manages state transitions, selects reasoning techniques, and coordinates component interactions. A core engine implements the underlying state machine: it tracks reasoning iterations, evaluates convergence criteria, and determines when accumulated confidence suffices to produce a result. An event-driven architecture ensures that every reasoning stage emits observable events, enabling asynchronous processing, loose coupling, and complete audit trails.

A critique-driven refinement engine iteratively improves reasoning outputs~\cite{Reflexion, SelfRefine}. When the system detects logical flaws or low confidence conclusions, the refiner selects alternative reasoning techniques, retrieves additional context via RAG, or invokes different models. The model selector chooses the best model for each reasoning step based on task characteristics, historical accuracy, cost budget, and latency constraints; it maintains per-model, per task type performance profiles updated after every invocation. A scoring system evaluates output quality along four axes: factual accuracy, logical consistency, thoroughness, and confidence, and the consensus mechanism aggregates scores from parallel reasoning paths to produce a balanced conclusion.

The RAG subsystem~\cite{RAG} enriches model responses with grounded evidence from a knowledge base. It follows the standard pipeline of query expansion and intent normalization, dense embedding via the shared embedding engine~\cite{DPR}, approximate nearest-neighbor retrieval over a pre-built vector index, re-ranking by recency and source authority, and context injection into a structured prompt template~\cite{RAG}. Multi-turn conversational context is maintained by summarizing older exchanges and prioritizing recent or high-relevance history.

Recent self-reflective RAG methods~\cite{SelfRAG_ICLR24} and iterative retrieval generation loops~\cite{IterativeRAG_EMNLP23} extend this baseline by enabling the system to evaluate retrieved evidence critically and adaptively refine retrieval strategies based on generation quality.

The Universal AI Protocol layer defines a provider-agnostic communication abstraction that decouples application logic from the heterogeneous APIs of individual AI providers. The protocol standardizes message formats through a canonical request/response schema that maps to provider-specific payloads, and defines a uniform error taxonomy that translates provider-specific error codes into semantic categories (rate limit, authentication, model-unavailable, etc.). It also governs the interaction lifecycle, session initiation, streaming, and termination, so that these contracts remain consistent regardless of the underlying provider.

The protocol enables client applications to target a single interface while the underlying adapters (Section~\ref{sec:arch}) handle translation. This separation supports runtime provider substitution, multi-provider fan out for consensus, and transparent failover without changes to application code. The canonical schema follows a JavaScript Object Notation (JSON) based structure comprising header metadata (session ID, tenant ID, routing hints) and a typed payload envelope.

\section{Algorithmic Framework}
\label{sec:algo}

Task decomposition, model selection, parallel inference, and consensus are handled by a collection of coordinated algorithms that make up the GAIOL reasoning and orchestration pipeline. This section formalizes the pipeline that takes a user query from decomposition through parallel inference to consensus-aggregated output. We first give a phase overview, then present the orchestration and decomposition algorithms, and then the consensus mechanism and complexity.

\subsection{Phase Overview}

The reasoning process is divided into five phases that run in a fixed order but can adapt their internal behavior based on the query and context:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Decomposition: The input query $Q$ is decomposed into a set of dependent sub tasks $T = \{t_1, t_2, \ldots, t_n\}$ using a few-shot reasoning template.
    \item Smart Orchestration: Each subtask$t_i$ is mapped to a subset of models $\mathcal{M} \subset \mathcal{R}$ (Model Registry) based on capability and cost efficiency.
    \item Heterogeneous Inference: Models $m \in \mathcal{M}$ generate parallel candidate responses $C_i = \{c_{i,1}, c_{i,2}, \ldots\}$.
    \item Consensus Aggregation: A scoring function $f_{\text{score}}(C_i)$ evaluates internal consistency and cross-model agreement to select or synthesize the optimal path.
    \item Verification: The final synthesis is validated against the retrieved ground-truth from the Persistent Storage layer.
\end{enumerate}

The core orchestration procedure is a multi-agent beam search over reasoning paths. Algorithm~\ref{alg:orchestration} presents the core orchestration logic, which employs a beam search strategy (ReAct: reasoning and acting)~\cite{ReAct} to explore multiple reasoning paths simultaneously while pruning suboptimal candidates at each step.

\begin{algorithm}[!ht]
\caption{GAIOL Intelligent Orchestration (Multi-Agent Beam Search)}
\label{alg:orchestration}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Query $Q$, Beam Width $k$, Model Registry $\mathcal{R}$
\Ensure Optimized Response $S$

\State $T \gets \text{Decompose}(Q)$ \Comment{$T = \{t_1, t_2, \ldots, t_n\}$}
\State $B \gets \{ \emptyset \}$ \Comment{Initialize beams with empty paths}

\For{each step $t_i \in T$}
    \State $C \gets \emptyset$ \Comment{Candidate list for current step}
    \For{each path $p \in B$}
        \State $\mathcal{M} \gets \text{SelectModels}(t_i, \mathcal{R})$
        \State $O \gets \text{ParallelExecute}(\mathcal{M}, t_i, \text{Context}(p))$
        \State $O' \gets \text{ScoreAndRank}(O, \text{Objective}(t_i))$
        
        \If{ConsensusEnabled}
            \State $O' \gets \text{ApplyConsensus}(O')$
        \EndIf
        
        \For{each candidate $o \in O'$}
            \State $C \gets C \cup \{ p \parallel o \}$ \Comment{Extend path}
        \EndFor
    \EndFor
    \State $B \gets \text{TopK}(C, k)$ \Comment{Prune to best $k$ paths}
\EndFor

\State $bestPath \gets \text{Top1}(B)$
\State $S \gets \text{Assemble}(bestPath)$
\State \Return $S$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

The following algorithm descriptions cover task decomposition, the ABTC consensus mechanism, and model selection. Complexity is summarized in a separate subsection.

\subsection{Algorithm Descriptions and Complexity Analysis}

Algorithm~\ref{alg:decomposition} describes the decomposition process that transforms complex queries into structured sub-tasks with dependency tracking. Algorithm~\ref{alg:consensus} introduces the \emph{Adaptive Bayesian Trust-Weighted Consensus} (ABTC) mechanism, a novel contribution of this work that dynamically learns per-model, per-domain trust scores using Bayesian posterior updates and applies them during multi-model response aggregation. Algorithm~\ref{alg:selection} describes the strategy-based model selection that routes tasks to appropriate models based on task characteristics and model capabilities.

\begin{algorithm}[!ht]
\caption{Dynamic Task Decomposition}
\label{alg:decomposition}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Query $Q$, Decomposition Template $\mathcal{P}$
\Ensure Ordered task list $T = \{t_1, t_2, \ldots, t_n\}$

\State $prompt \gets \text{FormatTemplate}(\mathcal{P}, Q)$
\State $response \gets \text{LLMInference}(prompt)$
\State $steps \gets \text{ParseJSON}(response)$

\If{$steps = \emptyset$ \textbf{or} $\neg\text{ValidateSteps}(steps)$}
    \State $steps \gets \text{FallbackDecomposition}(Q)$ \Comment{See below}
\EndIf

\For{$i \gets 1$ \textbf{to} $|steps|$}
    \State $t_i.\textit{id} \gets i$
    \State $t_i.\textit{dependencies} \gets \text{InferDependencies}(steps, i)$
    \State $t_i.\textit{objective} \gets steps[i].\textit{objective}$
    \State $t_i.\textit{taskType} \gets \text{ClassifyTask}(steps[i])$
\EndFor

\State $T \gets \text{TopologicalSort}(\{t_1, \ldots, t_n\})$
\State \Return $T$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

When the LLM-based decomposition fails validation (empty or malformed output), \textsc{FallbackDecomposition} applies a fixed seven-step template: (1)~problem statement extraction, (2)~constraint identification, (3)~approach selection, (4)~step-by-step execution, (5)~intermediate verification, (6)~synthesis, and (7)~confidence assessment. This template was derived empirically from the most common decomposition patterns observed during development and guarantees that every query produces at least a minimal task graph.

\begin{algorithm}[!ht]
\caption{Adaptive Bayesian Trust-Weighted Consensus (ABTC)}
\label{alg:consensus}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Candidate responses $C = \{c_1, \ldots, c_m\}$, Trust matrix $\mathcal{T} = \{(\alpha_m^{(d)}, \beta_m^{(d)})\}$, Task domain $d$, Decay factor $\lambda$
\Ensure Selected response $c^*$, Confidence $\sigma$, Updated $\mathcal{T}$

\For{each candidate $c_i \in C$}
    \State $m_i \gets \text{source}(c_i)$ \Comment{Originating model}
    \State $\hat{\tau}_i \gets \alpha_{m_i}^{(d)} / (\alpha_{m_i}^{(d)} + \beta_{m_i}^{(d)})$ \Comment{Posterior trust mean}
    \State $s_i^{\text{quality}} \gets \text{EvaluateQuality}(c_i, Q)$
    \State $s_i^{\text{agree}} \gets \text{CrossModelAgreement}(c_i, C \setminus \{c_i\})$
    \State $s_i \gets w_q \cdot s_i^{\text{quality}} + w_a \cdot s_i^{\text{agree}} + w_t \cdot \hat{\tau}_i$ \Comment{Trust-weighted score}
\EndFor

\State $C' \gets \text{SortDescending}(C, s)$
\State $c^* \gets C'[1]$
\State $\sigma \gets s_1 / \sum_{j=1}^{m} s_j$

\If{$\sigma < \theta_{\min}$}
    \State $c^* \gets \text{SynthesizeResponse}(C'[1:3])$
\EndIf

\Statex \Comment{\textbf{Bayesian trust update with temporal decay}}
\For{each model $m_i$ that contributed a candidate}
    \State $\alpha_{m_i}^{(d)} \gets \lambda \cdot \alpha_{m_i}^{(d)} + \mathbb{1}[m_i = m_w]$ \Comment{Reward winner}
    \State $\beta_{m_i}^{(d)} \gets \lambda \cdot \beta_{m_i}^{(d)} + \mathbb{1}[m_i \neq m_w]$ \Comment{Penalise non-winner}
\EndFor

\State \Return $c^*, \sigma, \mathcal{T}$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\begin{algorithm}[!ht]
\caption{Strategy-Based Model Selection}
\label{alg:selection}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Task $t$, Model Registry $\mathcal{R}$, Budget $b$
\Ensure Selected models $\mathcal{M} \subseteq \mathcal{R}$

\State $taskType \gets \text{ClassifyTask}(t)$
\State $complexity \gets \text{EstimateComplexity}(t)$
\State $candidates \gets \emptyset$

\For{each model $m \in \mathcal{R}$}
    \If{$m.\textit{capabilities} \cap taskType \neq \emptyset$}
        \State $score \gets \text{ComputeFitness}(m, t, complexity)$ \Comment{Eq.~\ref{eq:fitness}}
        \State $cost \gets \text{EstimateCost}(m, t)$
        \If{$cost \leq b$}
            \State $candidates \gets candidates \cup \{(m, score, cost)\}$
        \EndIf
    \EndIf
\EndFor

\State $\mathcal{M} \gets \text{SelectDiverseTop}(candidates, k_{\text{models}})$
\State \Return $\mathcal{M}$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\noindent\textsc{ComputeFitness} scores each candidate model as a weighted combination of three signals:


\begin{equation}
\begin{split}
\text{fitness}(m, t) = w_c \cdot \text{CapMatch}(m, t) + w_h \cdot \text{HistAcc}(m, \text{type}(t)) \\
 \quad + w_e \cdot (1 - \hat{c}_m)
\end{split}
\label{eq:fitness}
\end{equation}
\noindent where $\text{CapMatch}$ is the Jaccard overlap between the model's declared capabilities and the task requirements, $\text{HistAcc}$ is the model's historical accuracy for the classified task type (drawn from the Performance Tracker), and $\hat{c}_m$ is the model's normalized per-token cost. We set $w_c = 0.4$, $w_h = 0.4$, $w_e = 0.2$ throughout the experiments. For ABTC composite scoring (Algorithm~\ref{alg:consensus}), we set $w_q = 0.5$, $w_a = 0.3$, and $w_t = 0.2$.

\noindent\textsc{SelectDiverseTop} selects the top-$k_{\text{models}}$ candidates by fitness while enforcing provider diversity: no more than $\lceil k_{\text{models}}/2 \rceil$ models may come from the same provider, preventing the selection set from collapsing to a single API. In the experiments reported here, $k_{\text{models}} = 3$ and the beam width for the orchestration algorithm (Algorithm~\ref{alg:orchestration}) is $k = 3$. A sensitivity analysis for both $\lambda$ and $k$ is reported in Section~\ref{sec:sensitivity}.

\subsection{Adaptive Bayesian Trust-Weighted Consensus}
\label{sec:abtc}

Existing multi-model orchestration frameworks employ static weight vectors for response aggregation~\cite{AutoGen, MACollab}. Such vectors cannot adapt to model drift, provider outages, or domain-specific performance asymmetries observed in heterogeneous LLM deployments. We propose \emph{Adaptive Bayesian Trust-Weighted Consensus} (ABTC), a novel consensus mechanism that learns per-model, per-domain trust scores online through Bayesian posterior updates.

Let $\mathcal{R} = \{m_1, \ldots, m_M\}$ denote the model registry and $\mathcal{D} = \{d_1, \ldots, d_D\}$ the set of task domains (e.g., analytical reasoning, code generation, creative synthesis). For each model, domain pair $(m, d)$, we maintain a Beta-distributed trust variable:

\begin{equation}
\tau_m^{(d)} \sim \text{Beta}(\alpha_m^{(d)},\; \beta_m^{(d)})
\label{eq:trust_prior}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $\alpha_m^{(d)}$ and $\beta_m^{(d)}$ accumulate success and failure counts respectively. The posterior mean $\hat{\tau}_m^{(d)} = \alpha_m^{(d)} / (\alpha_m^{(d)} + \beta_m^{(d)})$ serves as the point estimate of model $m$'s reliability for domain $d$. New models are initialised with a non-informative prior $\text{Beta}(1,1)$, which is equivalent to a uniform distribution over $[0,1]$.

After each consensus round in domain $d$, the winning model $m_w$ receives a success signal while non-selected models receive a failure signal:

\begin{equation}
\alpha_{m}^{(d)} \leftarrow \lambda\,\alpha_{m}^{(d)} + \mathbb{1}[m = m_w], \qquad
\beta_{m}^{(d)}  \leftarrow \lambda\,\beta_{m}^{(d)}  + \mathbb{1}[m \neq m_w]
\label{eq:update}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $\lambda \in (0,1)$ is a temporal decay factor that down-weights historical observations to handle model updates, API changes, and concept drift~\cite{ThompsonSampling}. Setting $\lambda = 1$ recovers the standard Beta-Bernoulli conjugate update. In practice, we use $\lambda = 0.98$, providing an effective memory window of approximately 50 recent interactions.

Let $p_m^{(d)}$ denote the true probability that model $m$ produces the highest-quality response for domain $d$. When $\lambda = 1$ (no decay), the ABTC trust estimates converge almost surely to the true reliability as the number of consensus rounds $n \to \infty$:

\begin{equation}
\hat{\tau}_m^{(d)} \xrightarrow{\;a.s.\;} p_m^{(d)} \qquad (\lambda = 1)
\label{eq:convergence}
\end{equation}

\noindent This follows directly from the strong law of large numbers applied to the Beta posterior~\cite{BetaBernoulli}. For $\lambda < 1$ (the practically used setting), classical convergence does not hold: the effective sample size is bounded by $1/(1 - \lambda)$, inducing a non-vanishing posterior variance of order $O(1-\lambda)$. The trust means, therefore, tracks a moving average of recent model performance rather than converging to a fixed point. This is by design: the non-vanishing variance enables continual adaptation to non-stationary environments, such as provider-side model updates, at the cost of pointwise precision, analogous to the exploration, exploitation balance in Thompson Sampling~\cite{ThompsonSampling}.

The uniform prior $\text{Beta}(1,1)$ ensures that new models are neither favoured nor penalised. During the first $N_{\text{warm}} = \lceil 1/(1-\lambda) \rceil$ interactions, the system operates in an exploration phase where the trust component $w_t \cdot \hat{\tau}_i$ contributes equally for all models, and selection is dominated by the quality and agreement signals ($w_q$ and $w_a$ terms in Algorithm~\ref{alg:consensus}). The posterior concentrates after approximately $2 \cdot N_{\text{warm}}$ observations, after which the trust signal provides meaningful discrimination.

Because ABTC updates trust scores based on the selected response, a potential circularity arises: if the scoring function systematically favours certain response characteristics (e.g., verbosity), the trust scores could converge to reward those characteristics rather than true quality. We address this concern through three mechanisms.

First, \textsc{EvaluateQuality}$(c_i, Q)$ is a deterministic, reference-free function computed entirely from the candidate response $c_i$ and the original query $Q$, with no dependence on other candidates or on prior trust scores. It is defined as a weighted combination of three locally computed sub-signals: (1)~\emph{Semantic Relevance} ($w=0.4$): cosine similarity between dense vector embeddings of $c_i$ and $Q$, computed using the \texttt{all-MiniLM-L6-v2} local embedding model; (2)~\emph{Lexical Coverage} ($w=0.3$): unigram $F_1$ overlap between the candidate and TF-IDF keywords extracted from the query; and (3)~\emph{Structural Completeness} ($w=0.3$): a rule-based check for answer-closure markers and response length relative to query complexity. When the embedding model is unavailable, the function falls back to Jaccard token overlap.

Second, \textsc{CrossModelAgreement}$(c_i, C \setminus \{c_i\})$ is defined as the mean pairwise cosine similarity between the embedding of $c_i$ and the embeddings of all other candidates in the ensemble. This metric is computed independently of any prior selection or trust score.

Third, a weight-dominance analysis confirms that trust alone cannot override the quality and agreement signals: since $w_q + w_a = 0.8$ versus $w_t = 0.2$, a model with poor quality and agreement scores cannot win simply by accumulating high trust. Furthermore, the exponential decay bound $\lambda^{50} \approx 0.36$ and $\lambda^{100} \approx 0.13$ demonstrates that any single biased selection's influence is strictly bounded and diminishing.

Empirically, the ablation study in Section~\ref{sec:abtc_ablation} confirms that ABTC does not degenerate: trust posteriors diverge across domains (e.g., GPT-4 at $\hat{\tau} \approx 0.82$ for analytical tasks but $0.61$ for creative tasks), indicating that the mechanism tracks genuine performance variation rather than converging to a fixed bias. External cross-validation using human preference labels further confirms this finding (Section~\ref{sec:learning_curves}).

\section{Operational Flow}
\label{sec:flow}
\FloatBarrier

This section traces a query through GAIOL's end-to-end pipeline, from authentication to response delivery. We describe request intake and classification, then orchestration and consensus, then background operations.

\subsection{Request Pipeline: Intake, Classification, and Retrieval}

Every interaction begins at the web client, which validates user input and packages it with session identifiers, feature flags, and configuration parameters before forwarding the bundle to the API gateway (Figure~\ref{fig:request_intake}).

    \centering

    }{%
        \fbox{\parbox{\textwidth}{Missing file: \texttt{\detokenize{figs/Fig5.png}}}}%

    \caption{Request intake and authentication flow. The API gateway validates tokens, loads session history for returning users, and routes valid requests to the orchestrator.}
    \label{fig:request_intake}
\end{figure}

At the gateway, authentication middleware validates the bearer token against the identity provider and attaches the verified user context (organization, permissions, policy constraints); failed requests are rejected immediately. The routing layer classifies the request: conversational and reasoning queries proceed to the orchestrator, while administrative requests go to specialized control services. For returning users, the system loads conversation history and session-specific settings from persistent storage; new interactions receive a fresh session with default organizational preferences.

The orchestrator then scores the query on structural complexity, domain specificity, length, and dependence on prior context. Simple factual queries take a lightweight path (single-model, no decomposition); complex or multi-step queries trigger the full pipeline. For the latter, the RAG subsystem refines the query through entity extraction, semantic expansion, and intent normalization~\cite{RAG}, projects it into a dense vector~\cite{DPR}, and retrieves the top-ranked document chunks from the vector index via approximate nearest-neighbor search. These fragments are injected into a structured prompt template alongside conversation history and system instructions, grounding downstream model responses in retrieved evidence.

The next stage is orchestration, consensus, and response delivery.

\subsection{Orchestration, Consensus, and Delivery}

Complex queries are decomposed into coherent subtasks with dependency tracking (Algorithm~\ref{alg:decomposition}). The model selector ranks candidates by task fit, historical performance, cost, latency, and availability, then dispatches subtasks to the selected models in parallel (Figure~\ref{fig:orchestration}).
\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \IfFileExists{figs/Fig6.png}{%
        \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figs/Fig6.png}%
    }{%
        \fbox{\parbox{\textwidth}{Missing file: \texttt{\detokenize{figs/Fig6.png}}}}%
    }
    \caption{Multi-model orchestration flow. Subtasks are sequenced and routed to multiple models; a critic scores outputs and triggers re-selection until quality thresholds are met.}
    \label{fig:orchestration}
\end{figure}

Provider-specific adapters invoke the models asynchronously. Each response is normalized into a canonical format and annotated with execution metadata (latency, token count, error signals). A critical component~\cite{Reflexion} evaluates each response for factual accuracy, logical coherence, completeness, and consistency with retrieved context. When multiple responses are available, the ABTC consensus mechanism (Section~\ref{sec:abtc}) aggregates them through trust-weighted fusion~\cite{MACollab}. If quality falls below a configurable threshold, the system triggers refinement cycles: re-prompting with alternative models, retrieving additional context, or reformulating the query~\cite{SelfRefine}. Validated subtask outputs are assembled into a single coherent response.

Token consumption is metered at every model call and aggregated hierarchically (query, session, user, organization) using provider-specific pricing tables, enabling budget-constraint enforcement at each level. The final response, comprising generated content, citations, execution metadata, and a cost summary, is delivered as a structured JSON payload through the API gateway. When streaming is enabled, partial results are sent incrementally. The full session state is persisted for auditing, analytics, and future optimization.

Activities that run outside the request path are described next.

\subsection{Background Operations}

Outside the request path, background processes periodically review aggregate metrics to refine routing strategies and hyperparameters. A document ingestion pipeline handles chunking, embedding, and incremental knowledge-base updates; the model registry is refreshed whenever new models appear or pricing changes. Administrative functions (user management, policy configuration, model availability) run asynchronously. The error-handling subsystem classifies failures (transient, authentication, configuration, resource exhaustion) and applies recovery strategies (retry with backoff, failover routing, graceful degradation). Checkpointed session state enables recovery from partial failures, and real-time dashboards surface performance, usage, and cost metrics with alerting for anomalous conditions.

\subsection{Experimental Setup}

Quality metrics (relevance, coherence, completeness, accuracy, creativity) were assessed using a commercial frontier language model accessed via API as an automated evaluator, following established LLM-as-judge protocols~\cite{HolisticEval}. For each system response, the evaluator received a structured evaluation prompt containing the original query, the system's response, and a scoring rubric defining each dimension on a 0.0--1.0 scale with anchor descriptions (e.g., relevance: 0.0~=~completely off-topic, 0.5~=~partially addresses the query, 1.0~=~directly and fully addresses all aspects; creativity: 0.0~=~no novel synthesis, 0.5~=~conventional but adequate framing, 1.0~=~insightful reframing or novel connections). The exact system prompt and per-query template issued to the evaluator are reproduced verbatim in Appendix~\ref{app:rubric}. The evaluator was instructed to provide dimension-level scores and a brief justification for each.

To significantly expand evaluator reliability, we increased human annotation from 50 responses (10\%) to 175 responses (35\% of the benchmark), with annotators blind to the source system. Inter-rater agreement between the human annotators yielded a Cohen's $\kappa$ of 0.76 (substantial agreement), and the Pearson correlation between automated scores and the averaged human scores was $r = 0.84$ ($p < 0.001$), confirming the automated evaluator's alignment with human judgment.

To address the concern that consensus-synthesized responses may interact differently with the primary evaluator's stylistic preferences, we introduced a second independent evaluator from a different vendor (Gemini~1.5~Flash accessed via the Google AI API). Both evaluators scored the same 175 human-annotated responses; inter-evaluator agreement between the two automated judges was $r = 0.89$ ($p < 0.001$), with a mean absolute score difference of $0.031$. This cross-vendor agreement confirms that the quality scores are not artefacts of a single evaluator's preferences.

To situate GAIOL's capabilities, we compare against four baselines that represent progressively richer levels of orchestration:

Direct API (Sys-2). A single call to the same commercial frontier language-model API used for automated evaluation, with no orchestration, context management, or retrieval augmentation. This baseline isolates the raw model performance and serves as the lower bound.

LangChain (Sys-3). LangChain (v0.1)~\cite{LangChain} is a widely adopted open-source Python framework for composing LLM-based applications. We configured a LangChain agent with the ReAct (reasoning and acting)~\cite{ReAct} reasoning strategy and a dense vector retriever based on Facebook AI Similarity Search (FAISS)~\cite{DPR} over the same document corpus used by GAIOL. This baseline represents the current state-of-practice for single-agent, retrieval-augmented pipelines.

OpenRouter (Sys-4). OpenRouter is a commercial unified API gateway that routes requests to multiple LLM providers (OpenAI, Anthropic, Google, etc.) through a single endpoint. We used its default routing policy, which selects a model based on availability and cost. This baseline tests whether model-routing alone, without orchestration logic, suffices for complex tasks.

Multi-Wrapper (Sys-5). A custom Python wrapper that issues each query in parallel to two LLM providers (the same frontier model as Sys-2 and Gemini Pro), collects both responses, and selects the one with the higher self-reported confidence score. This baseline tests na\"ive multi-model coordination without structured reasoning, consensus mechanisms, or quality assessment.

\subsection{ABTC Ablation Study}
\label{sec:abtc_ablation}

To isolate the contribution of the Adaptive Bayesian Trust-Weighted Consensus mechanism (Section~\ref{sec:abtc}), we conduct an ablation study comparing three configurations of GAIOL's consensus layer:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Static-Equal: Uniform model weights ($\tau_m = 0.5$ for all $m$), equivalent to ablating the trust component entirely.
    \item Static-Tuned: Hand-tuned weights based on published benchmark scores (GPT-4: 0.8, Gemini Pro: 0.7, others: 0.5), representing the best-case static configuration.
    \item ABTC ($\lambda = 0.98$): The proposed adaptive mechanism with temporal decay.
\end{itemize}

All three configurations use identical quality evaluation and cross-model agreement functions; only the trust component $w_t \cdot \hat{\tau}_i$ differs. Results are reported over the same 500-query benchmark (10 runs each), as shown in Table~\ref{tab:abtc_ablation}.

\label{sec:autogen_protocol}

We evaluated AutoGen (v0.2) under two backend configurations over 100 analytical-reasoning and code-generation queries from the standard benchmark: (A)~AutoGen with GAIOL's unified API layer as the model backend (GAIOL-hosted), and (B)~AutoGen calling the OpenAI API directly (single-provider). \section{Open Challenges and Future Work}
\label{sec:gaps}

GAIOL's architecture includes extension points for capabilities, federation, governance, and adversarial robustness that the current evaluation does not cover. This section outlines the open problems that must be solved to realize those extensions. We group them by theme: semantic interoperability, adversarial robustness, governance and policy, federated scalability, and safe execution sandboxes.

\subsection{Semantic Standards for Multi-Agent Interoperability}

Existing agent frameworks define low-level transport (text passing, events, Representational State Transfer (REST) APIs)~\cite{AutoGen, CAMEL, MetaGPT}, but semantics remain implicit or framework-specific. Two independently built agents may share a transport protocol yet disagree on the meaning of a task description, a capability declaration, or a confidence score~\cite{dong2024large}. Cross-organizational orchestration (the core use case of GAIOL) requires agents to negotiate intent, context, and constraints without prior co-design. The research challenge is expressiveness versus generality: a rigid ontology limits what agents can represent, while a loose schema undermines coordination. Promising directions include capability-description languages, protocol-level contracts with formal preconditions, and shared task ontologies that permit local extensions.

\subsection{Adversarial Robustness in Open Multi-Agent Environments}

When GAIOL operates across partially trusted organizations, some agents may be compromised or deliberately adversarial~\cite{Weidinger, RedTeaming}. Attack surfaces range from poisoning the shared world model and injecting fabricated outputs into the consensus pipeline to exploiting orchestration scheduling logic or coordinating collusion across multiple agents. Static access-control lists and perimeter defenses cannot contain autonomous, adaptive entities that learn to act strategically. Defending against these threats requires continuous behavioral monitoring, anomaly-aware trust scoring (extending ABTC with adversarial detection signals), and isolation mechanisms that can quarantine suspicious agents without halting the entire pipeline. Designing orchestration strategies that remain robust under adaptive adversaries, especially when the adversaries themselves are learning agents, remains an open problem.

\subsection{Governance and Policy Enforcement at Scale}

GAIOL workflows span multiple clouds, legal jurisdictions, and administrative domains, each with distinct regulatory, ethical, and organizational constraints~\cite{FM_Governance, FedLearn}. Solaiman and Dennison outline considerations for generative-AI release and risk mitigation~\cite{SafetyTaxonomy_FAccT23}, and constitutional AI offers scalable alignment through AI feedback, but neither addresses the federated case where a single workflow must simultaneously satisfy conflicting data-residency laws in different countries. Current governance mechanisms are platform-specific and centralized, and thus unsuitable for ecosystems where no single party holds authority. Machine-readable policy languages, cross-domain negotiation protocols, and runtime enforcement engines that balance decentralization with accountability are needed but do not yet exist at production quality.

\subsection{Federated Scalability and Performance Trade-offs}

Federated execution enables data localization and regulatory compliance~\cite{FedLearn}, but coordination costs (communication latency, synchronization overhead, per-request security and governance checks~\cite{NISTZTA}) degrade throughput versus centralized deployments. Prior work shows distributed ML and auto-scaling are feasible~\cite{FedML_MLSys22, Autoscaling_MAS}, but combining auto-scaling with federated orchestration, where agents must respect jurisdiction-specific policies while maintaining global consensus, introduces compounding complexity that current systems do not handle well. Quantifying federation overhead under realistic adversarial and dynamic conditions remains an important next step.

\subsection{Standardized Sandboxes for Safe AI Execution}
Generic containerization (Docker, WebAssembly) provides process-level isolation but ignores AI-specific hazards such as uncontrolled tool use~\cite{Toolformer} and training-data leakage, as well as nondeterministic output and opaque decision logic~\cite{Weidinger}. No existing standard defines an AI sandbox that unifies execution isolation, fine-grained data and tool permissions, real-time behavior monitoring, and deterministic replay for auditing, GAIOL would benefit from such a standard: it would enable agents to migrate between platforms without per-deployment safety engineering and would give operators deterministic replay of agent actions for post-hoc auditing. Defining sandbox specifications that accommodate the diversity of AI workloads (from lightweight retrieval agents to graphics processing unit (GPU)-intensive model-serving pipelines) without imposing prohibitive overhead is an unsolved design problem.

\section{Limitations and Scope}
\label{sec:limitations}

We scope the current evaluation to isolate the architectural and algorithmic contributions of GAIOL; several deliberate design choices bound the claims that can be drawn. The following paragraphs state the main limitations.

We evaluate on 500 synthetically constructed queries spanning five domains, following the controlled-benchmark methodology standard in systems research~\cite{HolisticEval}. Synthetic queries allow precise control over domain balance, complexity distribution, and reproducibility, properties that production traffic does not guarantee. The benchmark covers the same task categories used by prior orchestration studies, and the full query set is publicly released for independent replication. To address the external validity concern, we additionally evaluated GAIOL directly on MMLU, HumanEval, and MT-Bench (see Table~\ref{tab:standard_benchmarks}), confirming that the quality advantages observed on the synthetic benchmark generalize to established suites.

Quality scores are produced by a GPT-4-based evaluation pipeline. To substantially reduce evaluator bias risk, we increased human annotation to 175 responses (35\%), obtained Cohen's $\kappa = 0.76$ (substantial agreement), and introduced Gemini~1.5~Flash as a second independent evaluator from a different vendor. The inter-evaluator agreement between the two automated judges was $r = 0.89$, confirming that the scores are not artifacts of a single evaluator's stylistic preferences. These mitigations are described in Section~\ref{sec:exp}; a residual risk from blended-phrasing preference remains and is reported transparently.

All experiments ran on a single Azure Standard\_D8s\_v3 instance to ensure reproducibility and fair comparison across baselines. The 200~req/s throughput and 5~ms orchestration overhead are therefore measured under controlled, single-node conditions. Because GAIOL's orchestration logic is stateless and horizontally partitionable, we expect these numbers to scale linearly with additional nodes; validating this under geo-distributed, multi-cloud conditions is planned for future work.

The ABTC convergence analysis (Section~\ref{sec:abtc}) assumes a locally stationary model quality distribution. The temporal decay factor ($\lambda = 0.98$, effective window $\approx$50 interactions) accommodates gradual model drift, which is the common case for provider-side updates. Abrupt model replacements, such as a silent GPT-4 revision, fall outside the stationarity assumption; incorporating change-point detection to trigger trust resets is a planned extension.

The current evaluation validates the orchestration pipeline and ABTC consensus mechanism, the components that this paper claims as contributions. Architectural provisions designed for future extension, including the federated data mesh, cross-organizational governance engine, and the continuous world model (see Table~\ref{tab:os_mapping}), are described as design-time abstractions but are not empirically evaluated in this work. Their validation under federated, multi-tenant, multi-cloud conditions is planned as a primary objective of subsequent deployments. Readers should therefore interpret the architectural sections as a design rationale, not as claims of demonstrated capability, for any component marked ``Designed'' in Table~\ref{tab:os_mapping}.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We presented GAIOL, a layered orchestration framework that coordinates heterogeneous LLM providers behind a uniform interface and aggregates their outputs through adaptive consensus. Unlike prior systems that address routing, memory, or agent communication in isolation, GAIOL combines beam-search task decomposition, strategy-based model selection, and retrieval-augmented context injection in a single pipeline. The architecture includes extension points for federated data access, cross-organizational governance, and shared world-state management, whose empirical validation is left to future work. The central algorithmic contribution is ABTC, which replaces static consensus weights with per-model, per-domain trust scores learned online through Beta-Bernoulli posterior updates. On a 500-query benchmark, ABTC delivers statistically significant quality gains over both uniform and hand-tuned baselines across all five task domains, with the largest improvements in code generation (+9~pp) and creative synthesis (+7~pp). The full GAIOL pipeline achieves an overall quality score of 0.83, a 95.2\% success rate, and 5~ms orchestration overhead, demonstrating that multi-model consensus can be practical without prohibitive latency or cost.

Several directions remain open. Evaluating the architectural provisions for federation and governance under multi-tenant, multi-cloud conditions is the next step. Standardized semantic protocols are needed for cross-framework agent interoperability. Adversarial robustness in partially trusted multi-agent environments requires continuous trust monitoring beyond what ABTC currently provides.

\section{Benchmark Construction}
\label{app:benchmark}
The evaluation uses a fixed 500-query benchmark spanning five domains---analytical reasoning, code generation, multi-step problem solving, knowledge retrieval, and creative synthesis---with 100 queries per domain. Within each domain, queries are stratified by complexity in a \textbf{2:5:3} ratio (simple:intermediate:complex). The complete query set, domain metadata, and tier assignments are defined in \texttt{scripts/benchmark/run\_benchmark.ts} (constant \texttt{DOMAINS}) in the public replication repository.

\section{Representative Query--Response Pairs}
\label{app:examples}
Three representative query--response pairs per domain (one per complexity tier) are provided in the replication repository rather than inlined here. Full traces---including responses, consensus confidence ($\hat{\tau}$), and per-dimension quality scores from the dual-vendor LLM-as-judge evaluator (Section~\ref{sec:exp})---are available in \texttt{scripts/benchmark/results/benchmark\_results.json}.

\section{Evaluation Protocol and LLM-as-Judge Rubric}
\label{app:rubric}

Five dimensions are scored in $[0,1]$ with anchors at 0.0, 0.5, and 1.0; overall quality is the unweighted mean. Table~\ref{tab:rubric} summarises the rubric. The judge prompt below matches \texttt{orchestrator/src/evaluation/scorer.ts} (truncated for space). The cross-vendor evaluator and human annotators ($n=175$) used the same anchor descriptions.

\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\caption{LLM-as-judge quality rubric (anchors per dimension)}
\label{tab:rubric}
\small
\resizebox{\linewidth}{!}{%
\begin{tabular}{@{}>{\raggedright\arraybackslash}p{0.13\linewidth}>{\raggedright\arraybackslash}p{0.27\linewidth}>{\raggedright\arraybackslash}p{0.27\linewidth}>{\raggedright\arraybackslash}p{0.27\linewidth}@{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Dimension} & \textbf{0.0 (fails)} & \textbf{0.5 (partial)} & \textbf{1.0 (meets)} \\
\midrule
Relevance & Completely off-topic & Partially addresses query & Directly and fully addresses all aspects \\
Coherence & Internally contradictory & Mostly consistent with minor gaps & Fully consistent and well-structured \\
Completeness & Ignores major aspects & Addresses main aspects but misses some & Covers all required aspects \\
Accuracy & Clearly factually wrong & Mostly correct with minor errors & Factually correct and grounded \\
Creativity & No novel synthesis & Conventional but adequate framing & Insightful reframing or novel connections \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}}
\end{table}

\textbf{Judge prompt (system excerpt):}
\begin{quote}\small
\begin{verbatim}
You are an impartial evaluator of AI-generated responses. Score the
response on five dimensions using the rubric below. Return a JSON object
with keys: relevance, coherence, completeness, accuracy, creativity,
justification.

Rubric (0.0 = fails criterion, 0.5 = partially meets, 1.0 = fully meets):
- relevance: 0.0=completely off-topic, 0.5=partially addresses query,
  1.0=directly and fully addresses all aspects
- coherence: 0.0=internally contradictory, 0.5=mostly consistent with
  minor gaps, 1.0=fully consistent and well-structured
[...]
- creativity: 0.0=no novel synthesis, 0.5=conventional but adequate
  framing, 1.0=insightful reframing or novel connections

Query: {query}
Response: {response}
JSON scores:
\end{verbatim}
\end{quote}
